# Developmental Psychology Review: The Big Wobbly Book of Skateboarding

**Reviewer:** Child psychologist specializing in ages 6-12, growth mindset, resilience, fear/anxiety management, and motor skill development

**Date:** February 2026

**Overall Assessment:** This is an exceptionally well-written children's instructional book that handles the psychology of learning, failing, and persisting with more sophistication and care than most books explicitly about those topics. The emotional architecture of the book, moving from equipment to safety to skill to struggle to community to parental support, mirrors the actual emotional arc a child experiences when learning a physically demanding skill. My concerns are minor. This book gets the hard stuff right.

---

## Developmental Appropriateness of Language and Concepts

The reading level sits comfortably in the 8-12 range, with enough humor and visual anchoring (the illustration notes suggest strong image support) that engaged 6-7 year olds could access it with a parent reading along. This is appropriate for a book that explicitly addresses both kids and their adults.

**Strengths:**

- The conversational, second-person voice ("I know. I see you eyeing it.") creates intimacy without condescension. Children in this age range are acutely sensitive to being talked down to, and this book consistently treats them as capable people who deserve real information. That respect is developmentally on-target and builds intrinsic motivation.

- Technical information (7-ply maple, ASTM F1492, PEACE & LOVE protocol) is presented without apology or dumbing down, but always with enough context that the child understands *why* it matters. This models intellectual curiosity beautifully.

- The humor functions as emotional regulation. Jokes appear most densely in sections that might otherwise trigger anxiety (the safety chapter, the falling chapter). This is not accidental, and it works. Humor lowers cortisol and increases receptivity to new information, especially in anxious learners.

**One concern:**

The phrase "your instinct will be to catch yourself with your hands. This is a terrible instinct, but it is a very powerful one" (Ch 2, wrist guards section) uses the word "terrible" to describe something the child's body does automatically. For anxious children, being told their instincts are "terrible" can increase body distrust. Consider something like "your instinct will be to catch yourself with your hands. This instinct is very strong, but it's not the safest one." The information stays the same; the framing shifts from judgment of the child's body to a neutral observation.

## Fear, Failure, and Frustration (Chapter 7 Deep Dive)

Chapter 7 is the emotional core of this book, and it is remarkably well done. I want to call out several things that are psychologically precise.

**"You don't have to get back on immediately."**

This is the single most important sentence in the book from a psychological standpoint. The dominant cultural narrative around resilience, especially in sports, is "get back on the horse." That narrative causes real harm. It teaches children that their emotional response to pain or fear is an obstacle to be overcome rather than information to be processed. This book explicitly rejects that framing. "Sit down. Breathe. Check yourself over... Sit for five minutes or twenty minutes or the rest of the day. Then come back tomorrow. The board will wait." This is textbook healthy coping. It validates the emotional response, provides concrete self-care steps, and removes the time pressure. Outstanding.

**"Comparison is a thief. Progress is personal."**

Developmentally, children ages 8-12 are entering what Erikson called the "industry vs. inferiority" stage, where social comparison becomes a primary way they evaluate their own competence. This sentence directly addresses that tendency without dismissing it. It does not say "don't compare yourself to others" (which children cannot do on command). It names comparison as a force that steals something from them, which gives the child agency to resist it.

**The fear section:**

"Fear is not weakness. Ignoring fear is not bravery. Real bravery is listening to the fear, making an informed decision, and acting accordingly. Sometimes bravery means dropping in. Sometimes bravery means walking away and coming back with more preparation. Both count."

This is an accurate and sophisticated model of courage that many adults have not internalized. It reframes fear as a cognitive tool rather than an emotional obstacle. For children developing their risk-assessment capabilities, this is exactly the right message. It avoids the two toxic poles: "just do it" (which teaches recklessness) and "be careful" (which teaches avoidance). Instead, it teaches *appraisal*, which is the actual psychological skill underlying healthy risk-taking.

**One area to consider strengthening:**

The "When You Feel Defeated" section addresses comparison and slow progress but does not explicitly name frustration as a feeling. Children in this age range often cannot distinguish between "I'm frustrated" and "I can't do this." Adding a brief acknowledgment that frustration is a sign your brain is working hard, not a sign you should stop, would complete the emotional toolkit. Something like: "That hot, angry feeling when you try something ten times and it still doesn't work? That's frustration. And frustration means your brain is working on the problem. It doesn't feel good, but it's actually a sign that you're learning." This aligns with research on "desirable difficulty" in learning.

## Growth Mindset Messaging: Authentic or Toxic Positivity?

This is where many children's books fail, and where this book succeeds decisively.

**What toxic positivity looks like:** "You can do anything if you believe in yourself!" "Every day is a great day!" "Just stay positive!"

**What this book does instead:**

- "It's going to be hard. You're going to fall. You're going to want to quit." (A Note Before We Begin) This is honest. It sets accurate expectations. Research on "mental contrasting" (Oettingen, 2014) shows that pairing optimism with realistic obstacles actually increases persistence compared to optimism alone.

- "The difference between the people who become skateboarders and the people who don't is not talent. It's stubbornness." This reframes success as a function of persistence rather than innate ability, which is the foundation of growth mindset. But it uses the word "stubbornness" rather than a softer word like "determination," which gives it an honesty and humor that children will trust more.

- "Progress is not linear. Some days they'll look amazing. Some days they'll look like they've never seen a skateboard before. Both are normal." (Ch 6, parent note) This normalizes regression, which is one of the most distressing aspects of skill acquisition for children. Many growth-mindset materials imply that effort always leads to visible improvement. This book correctly identifies that it does not, and frames that as part of the process rather than a failure of effort.

- The dedication: "For everyone who fell down and got back up. And for everyone who fell down and sat there for a while. That's okay too." This is the book's thesis statement, and it is psychologically sound. It validates both resilience AND rest. Most books only validate the first.

**Verdict:** The growth mindset messaging in this book is authentic, well-calibrated, and avoids the toxic positivity trap entirely. It is honest about difficulty, specific about what persistence looks like, and generous with permission to struggle.

## Parent/Guardian Messaging

The parent notes throughout the book and the dedicated Chapter 9 are strong. Let me address them section by section.

**Ch 1 (skateboard buying):** Practical and appropriate. No psychological concerns.

**Ch 2 (safety gear is not a negotiation):** "Please, please, please do not make safety gear a negotiation. It's not optional at any age. And the best way to make your kid wear gear without a fight? Wear some yourself if you're out there too. Model it. Make it normal. Make it boring. Boring is the goal." This is excellent parenting advice. Modeling is the most effective behavior-shaping tool available to parents, and the "boring" framing removes the power struggle dynamic that many families get stuck in.

**Ch 3 (first time on the board):** "'You look solid up there' is a good thing to say... 'Why aren't you moving yet?' is not." Providing specific scripts is enormously helpful for parents, especially anxious ones. This note correctly identifies that pressure to progress is the most common way well-meaning parents undermine their children's confidence.

**Ch 5 (first rides):** "Resist the urge to hover or grab them. If they fall, wait a beat, let them decide if they need help." This is developmentally critical. Children ages 6-12 are building autonomy and self-efficacy. A parent who rushes in after every stumble communicates "I don't think you can handle this," which directly undermines the confidence the activity is supposed to build.

**Ch 7 (hard falls/hard days):** "Don't say 'You're fine' (they might not be). Don't say 'Get back on' (they'll decide when). Do say: 'That looked rough. How are you feeling?'" This is the gold standard for emotional coaching. It validates the child's experience, asks rather than tells, and preserves the child's agency. The "you're fine" correction is especially important, as that phrase is one of the most common forms of inadvertent emotional invalidation parents use.

**Ch 8 (skatepark, older skaters):** "If an older skater gives your kid a tip, that's not a stranger being weird, that's skateboard culture." This is a valuable cultural translation for parents who may be unfamiliar with skatepark norms. It reduces parental anxiety without dismissing legitimate safety awareness.

**Chapter 9 (dedicated parent chapter):**

Strengths:
- "Let them fail" is framed as the hardest but most important job, which validates the difficulty parents feel while still directing them toward the right behavior.
- "Celebrate effort, not outcomes" is a direct application of Dweck's research on praise and motivation.
- "It's okay if they quit and come back" addresses a major source of parental anxiety and guilt. Many parents interpret a child's break from an activity as their own failure to motivate. This normalizes cycling in and out.

**One concern:**

The line "Don't push the cautious kid to go faster, and don't hold back the fearless kid (just make sure they're geared up)" is solid advice but could benefit from a brief acknowledgment that fearless kids sometimes need help learning to self-assess risk, not just gear. A fearless child who is never encouraged to pause and evaluate is building a habit of impulsivity, not courage. Something brief: "The fearless kid might need gentle help learning to stop and think, 'Am I ready for this?' before charging ahead. This is not discouragement. It is teaching them to think like a skilled skater."

## Language That Could Shame, Pressure, or Discourage

I reviewed the full manuscript specifically for language that could inadvertently cause harm. This book is remarkably clean. A few small items:

**1. "Mongo pushing" (Ch 5):**
"Some people do this (it's called 'mongo pushing') and while it works, it makes everything else harder later. Push with your back foot." The term "mongo" has mocking connotations in skate culture. A child who has been pushing mongo and reads this may feel embarrassed. The instruction to push with the back foot is correct, but I would add a normalizing sentence: "If you've been pushing mongo, no big deal, lots of people start that way. Just start practicing with your back foot and it'll feel natural soon." This prevents the child from feeling they've been "doing it wrong."

**2. "How hard can it be? Very. The answer is very." (Cover)**
This is funny and sets honest expectations. No concern here. I mention it only because some reviewers might flag it. Do not change it. Honest expectation-setting reduces anxiety and dropout.

**3. "Your backside, after you fall on it" (Ch 1, deck description):**
Mild body humor, appropriate for the age range. No concern.

**4. "Make S-turns without feeling like you're about to die" (Ch 6, Week 1-2 goals):**
This is hyperbolic humor that mirrors how kids actually talk about fear. It normalizes the intensity of the feeling. No concern.

**5. "This is going to feel ridiculous" (Ch 4, falling practice):**
This is good. It preemptively names the self-consciousness the child will feel, which reduces its power. Naming an emotion before it arrives is a validated anxiety-management technique (affect labeling).

## The Parent-Child Dynamic: Helicopter vs. Neglect Spectrum

This book navigates the helicopter-to-neglect spectrum with unusual skill. It does so by giving parents a clear role: **safety infrastructure provider and emotional witness, not coach or controller.**

The book consistently tells parents to:
- Ensure safety gear is worn (structure)
- Be present and attentive (witness)
- Avoid directing, correcting, or rushing (restraint)
- Validate feelings without fixing them (emotional coaching)
- Let the child set the pace (autonomy support)

This maps precisely onto what developmental psychology calls "authoritative parenting," the style associated with the best outcomes for child autonomy, competence, and emotional regulation. The book never uses that term, which is correct. It just models it.

The illustration note for the Ch 9 opener captures this perfectly: "A bigger Turtle (parent) sitting on a bench at the skatepark with a first aid kit and a slightly worried expression. Baby Turtle on a board nearby, having the absolute time of its life." The parent is present, prepared, and not intervening. This is the visual thesis of the book's parenting philosophy.

**One gap:**

The book does not address the parent who wants to skate with their child. This is increasingly common. A brief note in Chapter 9 about skating alongside your kid (rather than teaching/coaching them) could be valuable: shared activity without instructional pressure is one of the strongest bonding mechanisms available. Even a single line: "If you want to try it yourself, even better. Struggling alongside your kid is one of the most powerful things you can do. It shows them that adults fall down too, and that learning is a lifelong process."

## Final Notes

This book does something that is psychologically rare and valuable: it takes children's fear seriously without either dismissing it or amplifying it. It treats kids as people who deserve honest information, real skills, and the autonomy to make their own decisions about their own bodies and their own courage. The humor is not a coating over difficult material. It is woven into the teaching in a way that lowers defenses and increases absorption.

The emotional pacing is also worth noting. The book builds from concrete/practical (gear, parts) through physical skills (standing, pushing, falling) to emotional skills (fear, defeat, comparison) and finally to community (skatepark, parent relationship). This mirrors the actual developmental sequence a child goes through when picking up a new physical skill: first they need to feel safe, then competent, then emotionally resilient, then connected. Whether this structure was intentional or intuitive, it is correct.

The parent messaging is among the best I have seen in a children's activity book. Most books either ignore parents entirely or lecture them. This book speaks to parents as allies who are also scared, also learning, and also doing their best. That respect goes both ways, modeling for the child that their adults are human, and modeling for the adult that their child is capable.

I would recommend this book both as a skateboarding resource and as a model for how to talk to children about difficulty, risk, and persistence. The suggestions above are refinements, not corrections. The foundation is sound.
